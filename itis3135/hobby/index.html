Home

The hobby that I chose to build a site about is a bit unconventional, but what I chose is collecting mil-surp. Mil-surp stands for military surplus, and can be categorized in a few ways. The 2 big categories are firearms, and equipment. The latter is about items such as clothing, worn gear like vests and backpacks, rations, and packed gear such as bunking material and safety equipment. The Prior is about firearms that at one point were used in military service, but were phased out with better technology. Many are sold or given to smaller or developing countries until they could gather or build their own arms supply for their troops. After that, they are sold to be imported into the US commercial market to be sold commercially. Mil-surp is done both as a way to preserve historical items, as well as for governments to get more worth the phasing out of equipment than gained through the scrapping, trashing, or destruction of equipment. I am a part of the first group, having a collection of historical military firearms.

Where I Look

In the local Charlotte area, there aren't many gun stores, and the stores there are typically either pawn and gun stores, or are smaller stores with an indoor range. Because of this, there aren't really any stores that I have found with mil-surp in stock, so I go to online sources. I use a mixture of auction sites, as well as online stores to see what is available, and the worth and condition of items. I also use online resources to do research on any firearms that I plan to buy before purchasing. After purchase, firearms are shipped based laws and firearm classification, but for virtually all purchases* (except those exempted as antique through the US gun control act) are shipped to an FFL, or Federal Firearms License, where you undergo a background check by the licensed entity, and if you pass it, the firearm is transferred into your name. I typically will buy from online stores where I am getting "new" surplus, or surplus that is being sold on the commercial market for the first time.

When is the Best Time?

With military surplus, there is a lot of uncertainty with timing. By this, I mean that it cannot be known ahead of time what surplus is coming in, and when things will be in stock. Other times, there are many surplus rifles all coming at once. Of course every collector, including myself, has a list of firearms they want, but you can't easily collect exactly what you want whenever you want. The waiting time is spent enjoying what you already have, as well as learning about the guns you have and are looking for.


Example:

Say you're looking for a German Mauser, such as a k98, it will be hard to find, and probably very expensive. If you want to, you can wait, but if you don't mind doing some research, and a bit of compromising, you can find many k98s that were captured such as the Yugo k98, or even rifles made using captured machinery post war such as the Yugo M98. Other rifles, like the latter ones mentioned are more common, and are usually found in better condition at a lower price.

How I do it

Mil-surp collecting is a daunting hobby at first, but once you get a basic understanding, it isn't that bad. I am a history buff, so I like looking around at how these older weapons were thought up, and how they work. Because of this, as well as being tought how to properly handle firearms by my parents when I was younger, I can better understand what I am looking at. I enjoy the collection I have, and when I want to get a new addition for it, I look at the sites I mentioned on the "Where I Look" page. Because of my age, I am able to own rifles, but because of age laws in my state, I cannot own pistols, so I ignore pistols and only collect rifles. Typically, I will look through the surplus rifles on a few different sites, looking for any that I might like to get. If I find one, I look in the description and at the pictures in the listing to get an idea of the rifle's quality. If the quality is good, I check the price, to see if it is a fair price for what I think it is valued. I get my idea for value based on the quality I get from the last step, and the price I can find the same or similar rifles going for on other online stores and auctions. If I think it is a good buy, then I will purchase it and ship it to an FFL near me. I then research how to break down the rifle for cleaning, and any other information about the rifle. After passing the background check and getting the rifle, I break it down and inspect the condition, and usually clean it and check if it is fireable. If it is, I order ammo, and if applicable, clips or mags(and yes there is a difference).

Why do This?

I like collecting military surplus firearms because I love learning about development and the history behind them. For example, I got an M1 Carbine because I wanted to learn more about the type of rifle my great grandfather used in the second world war. I also love the beauty of older firearms with the woodworking involved in them, whereas most modern firearms use lighter weight and more durable materials. Don't get me wrong, I love modern firearms as well, and I think the technological advancements made are incredible, but the history of mil-surps is what draws me to it.
